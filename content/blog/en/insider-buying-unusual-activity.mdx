Most Form 4 filings are routine: option exercises, tax-driven sales under scheduled 10b5-1 plans, and RSU vests. The filing itself doesn't distinguish mechanical compensation activity from genuine insider conviction — that requires reading past the raw count to the type, timing, and pattern of the trade.

## Form 4 Is the Paper Trail — and It Has a Two-Day Lag Built In

SEC Form 4 must be filed within two business days of the transaction, per Rule 16a-3 under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. That short window is what makes Form 4 useful as a near-real-time signal compared to quarterly 13F filings, which can lag institutional activity by up to 45 days.

- Form 4 line item codes tell you what kind of transaction occurred: "P" is an open-market purchase, "S" is a sale, "M" is an option exercise, and "A" or "G" typically mark grants or awards — not open-market conviction buying.
- Form 4 itself does not state motive. Raw Form 4 counts without reading the transaction code overstate how much genuine signal is in the data.
- SEC EDGAR provides raw Form 4 access at zero cost, searchable by issuer CIK, within the same two-business-day window the law requires for filing.

## Why "Unusual" Volume Alone Is the Wrong Filter

Routine insider transactions — option exercises, tax-driven 10b5-1 sales, and scheduled RSU vests — dominate Form 4 filings by raw count. A large-looking Form 4 filing is not automatically a conviction signal; the transaction code and context matter more than the size.

- A meaningful "unusual" signal requires comparing an insider's current Form 4 against that same insider's own trading history, not against sector averages or headline share counts.
- Cluster purchases — multiple different insiders filing open-market buys within a few days of each other — are generally read as a stronger signal than one large single-insider transaction, because coordinated timing across separate people is harder to explain as routine, individually-scheduled compensation activity.
- A very large reported share or dollar figure on its own is not evidence of conviction — extreme outliers are more often a data or classification artifact (a corporate restructuring, share-class conversion, or index-related mechanical filing) than an organic open-market buy, and are worth double-checking against the raw SEC filing before treating them as a signal.

## Free Tools Catch the Filing — Paid Tools Add Context

SEC EDGAR's full-text search and company-filing pages are free and are the primary source — every other tracker is built on top of the same underlying data. Free aggregators like OpenInsider organize and filter EDGAR's raw Form 4 feed into a more readable format at no cost.

- Paid platforms generally layer on features EDGAR's raw interface doesn't provide: ratio-to-float context, an insider's historical trading baseline, and automatic tagging of scheduled 10b5-1 plan trades versus discretionary open-market buys.
- Whichever tool you use, the transaction code (P, S, M, A, G) on the underlying Form 4 is the ground truth — a dashboard's own labels and summary numbers are only as reliable as how carefully that tool applies those codes, so spot-checking a headline number against the actual EDGAR filing is worth the extra step before treating it as real.

## Legal vs Illegal Insider Trading — Why the Line Drives SEC Scrutiny

Legal insider trading covers every Form 4 filing: insider purchases, sales, option exercises, and grants, all disclosed under Section 16 of the Exchange Act. Illegal insider trading under SEC Rule 10b-5 requires trading on material non-public information (MNPI), which Form 4 filings by definition do not capture or prove on their own.

- Open-market insider buys that cluster within a few days of each other, especially ahead of earnings or M&A announcements, are the kind of pattern that historically draws closer regulatory attention — though the filing alone never establishes intent.
- Form 4 disclosure itself is not a defense against 10b-5 liability if a trade was actually sourced from MNPI before the filing window opened.

## FAQ

**What counts as "unusual" insider buying activity?**

Unusual activity is generally read through three filters together: size relative to the insider's own prior trading history (not sector averages), cluster timing of multiple different insiders filing within a few days of each other, and role weight — CEO, CFO, and director purchases carry more weight than VP-level filings. A single large trade from a non-C-suite insider, in line with their own past pattern, is rarely unusual. A cluster of several C-suite open-market buys within a short window is a stronger signal.

**Can I track insider buying in real time for free?**

Yes. SEC EDGAR publishes raw Form 4 filings within the two-business-day statutory window at zero cost, and free aggregators like OpenInsider organize that same data into a more searchable format. The trade-off with free tools is generally less historical benchmarking — you see the filing, but comparing it against an insider's own baseline pattern over time often takes manual work or a paid tool.

**Does heavy insider buying always mean the stock will go up?**

No. Academic research on insider trading (going back to studies like Seyhun's work in the late 1990s) has found that insider purchases outperform the market on average, but with wide dispersion, not as a reliable individual-stock signal. An unusually large single-name figure can also reflect a corporate restructuring, share-class conversion, or technical filing rather than organic conviction buying — worth checking the underlying SEC filing before treating it as a trading signal.

**How do I tell routine insider buying from truly unusual buying?**

Routine trades cluster around compensation events: option exercises (Form 4 code "M"), RSU vests, and scheduled 10b5-1 sales. Unusual trades appear as open-market purchases (code "P") that aren't tied to a scheduled plan. Comparing the current filing's size and timing against that same insider's own filing history over the past year or two is the clearest routine-versus-conviction separator.

**What does a cluster of purchases by multiple insiders indicate?**

A cluster of several different insiders filing open-market buys within a short window is generally read as harder to explain through independent, individually-scheduled compensation mechanics than a single large purchase. The caveat: clusters can also be triggered by one shared corporate event — a secondary offering, an index rebalance, or a compensation-plan change — that pushes multiple insiders to act around the same time without any shared private information involved.

**Why would an insider sell stock if the company is doing well?**

Insiders sell for reasons unrelated to outlook far more often than not: diversification, liquidity needs, estate planning, option expiration, or a 10b5-1 plan executing on a schedule set well before current performance. Insider buying is generally treated as the stronger signal of the two, because deploying personal capital is a voluntary choice, while selling is very often scheduled in advance.
