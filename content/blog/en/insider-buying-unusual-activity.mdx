## Legal vs Illegal Insider Trading — Why the Line Drives SEC Scrutiny

Legal insider trading covers every Form 4 filing: insider purchases, sales, option exercises, and grants, all disclosed under Section 16 of the Exchange Act. Illegal insider trading under SEC Rule 10b-5 requires trading on material non-public information (MNPI), which Form 4 filings by definition do not capture.

- Open-market insider buys that cluster within 0–5 days and precede earnings or M&A announcements historically attract SEC Division of Enforcement review.
- Form 4 disclosure itself is not a defense against 10b-5 liability if the trade was sourced from MNPI before the filing window opened.
- Cluster signals on platforms like UnderstandStock are what prompt retail aggregation, which in turn raises volume and can attract regulatory attention back to the original insiders.

## FAQ

**What counts as "unusual" insider buying activity?**

Unusual activity is defined by three filters applied together: dollar size relative to the insider's prior 12-month Form 4 history, cluster timing of multiple insiders filing within 0–5 days, and role weight (CEO, CFO, director purchases outweigh VP-level filings). A single large trade by a non-C-suite insider against their own historical baseline is rarely unusual. A cluster of three or more C-suite buys inside five days is.

**Can I track insider buying in real time for free?**

Yes. SEC EDGAR publishes raw Form 4 filings within two business days at zero cost. Insider Screener's free tier adds daily email reports, Excel export, and minute-level updates across 16 markets. OpenInsider aggregates free as well. The trade-off is no historical benchmarking: you see the filing, but not whether it deviates from the insider's own baseline without manual comparison.

**Does heavy insider buying always mean the stock will go up?**

No. Academic studies including Seyhun (1998) and Lakonishok & Lee (2001) show insider purchases outperform the market on average, but with significant dispersion. Single-name clusters like the $SVRE 6.4B-share net change can reflect corporate restructuring or share-class mechanics rather than conviction. The signal is statistically positive, not deterministic, and is weaker when insiders buy under 10b5-1 plans or option-exercise pressure.

**How do I tell routine insider buying from truly unusual buying?**

Routine trades cluster around compensation events: option exercises, RSU vests, 10b5-1 scheduled sales, and tax-driven diversification. Unusual trades appear as open-market buys on Form 4 line item "A" (direct purchase), not "M" (exercise) or "G" (grant). Compare the current filing's price, size, and timing against the insider's prior 24 months of Form 4 history. Cluster flags within 0 days are the clearest routine-vs-conviction separator.

**What does a cluster of purchases by multiple insiders indicate?**

A cluster indicates coordinated conviction that is hard to explain through independent compensation mechanics. UnderstandStock's cluster flag on $SVC at 0 days means multiple insiders filed within the same session, which historically outperforms single-insider buys in event studies. The risk: clusters can also be triggered by a single corporate event (secondary offering, index rebalance) that forces multiple insiders to act simultaneously, not by shared private information.

**Why would an insider sell stock if the company is doing well?**

Insiders sell for reasons unrelated to outlook: diversification, liquidity events, estate planning, option expiration, or 10b5-1 plan execution predating current performance. Insider buying is a stronger signal than insider selling because the decision to deploy personal capital is voluntary, while selling is often scheduled. Net-buy Form 4 activity is the metric that filters out mechanical selling.
