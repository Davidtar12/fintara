## Step 3 — Open and fund your brokerage account

Go to IBKR or Fidelity and start the account application. You will provide ID, proof of address, and tax information. Global investors can open IBKR accounts. The process takes 15 to 30 minutes. Link your bank account and initiate a transfer. ACH transfers in the US take 1 to 3 days. Wire transfers are faster but may have fees. Fund with at least $100 to buy one share of most dividend ETFs.

Enable two-factor authentication for security. Set your account to USD if you are buying US ETFs. Confirm the broker’s fee schedule. IBKR charges minimal commissions per trade. Fidelity offers zero-commission ETF trades. Check if your country imposes a foreign dividend tax. This affects your net return. The U.S. withholds 30% for non-residents unless a treaty reduces it.

## Step 4 — Place a buy order for your chosen ETF

Log into your brokerage and search for your chosen ticker. Start with VYM or SCHY for simplicity. Use a “market order” to buy at the current price. For example, if VYM is at $110, one share costs $110 plus any fee. Use a “limit order” if you want to set a max price. Place the order during market hours, 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM ET.

Confirm the trade and review the confirmation slip. The ETF will appear in your holdings. Set a dividend reinvestment plan (DRIP) if your broker offers it. This automatically buys more shares with payouts. Check the ex-dividend date to know when you qualify for the next payment. Dividends post to your cash balance. You can withdraw or reinvest them.

## Step 5 — Track performance and dividends

Open your brokerage dashboard weekly. Review the ETF’s price change and dividend history. Use the broker’s tools or a spreadsheet. Track total return, not just yield. Total return includes price change plus dividends. Compare your ETF to a benchmark like the S&P 500. The S&P is at 6,522.10; your ETF may lag or lead in different years.

Set calendar alerts for dividend payment dates. Most US dividend ETFs pay quarterly. SCHY and VYM typically pay in March, June, September, December. Log each payout and the reinvested shares. This builds your share count over time. Check the ETF’s expense ratio annually. Fees are deducted from the fund’s assets, not your account directly. A low ratio preserves your return.

## Common mistakes to avoid

Chasing high yield is the first mistake. A 6% yield may come from a falling stock price, not strong dividends. Stick to 2%–4% yields with low expense ratios. Ignoring taxes is the second mistake. Non-U.S. investors face 30% withholding on U.S. dividends. Check treaties and your local tax filing. Do not buy a dividend ETF in a taxable account if your country taxes dividends heavily.

Placing market orders during volatile hours is another error. Use limit orders to avoid bad fills. Do not ignore currency risk if you are not a USD-based investor. SCHY holds non-U.S. stocks, so your returns depend on USD strength. Finally, do not treat dividends as free money. They are a return of company profits. Reinvest them to compound growth.
